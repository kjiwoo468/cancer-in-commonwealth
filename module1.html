Section 3

What is cancer?

At its core, cancer is a disease of cells dividing when they shouldn't. Every cell in your body carries instructions — DNA — that tell it when to grow, when to divide, and when to die. Cancer happens when those instructions get damaged.

Step 1

A mutation occurs

A change (mutation) happens in a cell's DNA. Mutations can happen naturally, or be caused by things like sun exposure, tobacco smoke, or other substances.

Step 2

Controls break down

The mutation damages the cell's normal "off switches." Damaged cells that should die instead survive, and cells that aren't needed keep dividing anyway.

Step 3

A tumor can form

As these abnormal cells keep dividing, they can build up into a mass of tissue called a tumor. This can happen almost anywhere in the body.

Not every tumor is cancer — you'll compare the two main categories, benign and malignant, in a few sections.

Section 5

What is metastasis?

Sometimes, cancer cells break away from the tissue or organ where they started and travel — usually through the bloodstream or lymphatic system — to another part of the body. This spread is called metastasis.

A few key points about metastasis

  • Even after spreading, the cancer is still classified as the same type of cancer it started as. For example, breast cancer that spreads to the lungs is still called metastatic breast cancer — not lung cancer.
  • When and where a cancer spreads depends on the type of cancer and how quickly it's growing.
Section 6

Benign vs. malignant tumors

Not all tumors are cancer. Tumors generally fall into one of two categories:

Benign

  • Usually small
  • Not cancerous
  • Slow-growing
  • Non-invasive (doesn't grow into nearby tissue)
  • Cannot metastasize

Malignant

  • Often large
  • Cancerous
  • Fast-growing
  • Invasive (grows into nearby tissue)
  • Can metastasize

Quick check

True or false: A benign tumor can spread (metastasize) to other parts of the body.

Correct — false. By definition, benign tumors don't invade nearby tissue or spread to other parts of the body. That's one of the key things that separates them from malignant (cancerous) tumors.

Not quite. The statement is false. Benign tumors don't invade nearby tissue or spread to other parts of the body — that ability to invade and spread is one of the defining features of a malignant (cancerous) tumor.

Section 7

There are more than 100 types of cancer

"Cancer" isn't one disease — it's a category covering well over 100 different diseases. Most are named for the organ or tissue where they form, and some are named for the type of cell involved (for example, carcinoma, leukemia, and melanoma all describe cancers that start in particular cell types).

More common

Frequently diagnosed types

  • Breast
  • Cervical
  • Prostate
  • Colorectal
  • Liver
Less common

Rarer types

  • Ependymoma (a type of brain/spinal cord tumor)
  • Fallopian tube cancer
  • Heart cancer

Knowing that cancer comes in so many forms helps explain why there isn't a single "cure for cancer" — different cancers behave differently and often need very different approaches to treatment, which you'll dig into in Module 3.

Section 8

How is cancer diagnosed?

Sometimes cancer is found because a person notices symptoms. Other times, it's discovered during a routine screening — before a person notices anything at all. Either way, doctors follow a similar process to confirm a diagnosis.

1

Something is noticed

A routine screening detects an abnormality, or a patient reports symptoms (though sometimes there are no symptoms at all).

2

A biopsy is performed

A doctor performs a biopsy — removing a small sample of the tissue in question.

3

Cells are examined

The sample is examined under a microscope for the physical characteristics of cancerous cells.

Activity

Spot the difference: normal vs. cancer cells

When a pathologist looks at a tissue sample under a microscope, they're looking for differences in how the cells look and are arranged. Compare these two simplified illustrations of cell groups.

Make your call

Based on what a doctor looks for during a biopsy, which group shows cancer cells?

Correct — Group B. Cancer cells often look different from the healthy cells around them: irregular sizes and shapes, crowding and overlapping (a sign of uncontrolled division), and an unusually large nucleus compared to the rest of the cell (a higher nucleus-to-cytoplasm ratio). These are the kinds of features a pathologist looks for under a microscope after a biopsy.

Actually, it's Group B. Group A shows cells that are uniform and orderly — what you'd expect from healthy tissue. Group B shows the irregular sizes, shapes, crowding, and the larger nucleus-to-cytoplasm ratio that signal uncontrolled division — features a pathologist looks for after a biopsy.

Section 11

Who is most affected? Income and education

Research consistently shows a link between socioeconomic status and cancer outcomes:

  • People without a high school diploma have higher cancer rates than people who attended college.
  • Families earning less than $12,500 per year are about 1.7 times more likely to develop lung cancer than families earning $50,000 per year.
  • People in lower socioeconomic groups are also more likely to be diagnosed at more advanced stages of cancer.

Why might this be?

Less cancer-related knowledge

Limited exposure to health information can mean fewer people recognize early warning signs or know about screening recommendations.

Less access to healthcare

Cost, insurance coverage, and the availability of nearby providers all affect whether and when someone gets screened or treated.

Competing priorities

When resources are tight, healthcare — especially preventive care — can understandably take a back seat to more immediate needs.

Based on findings summarized in Clegg et al., "Impact of socioeconomic status on cancer incidence and stage at diagnosis," Cancer Causes & Control, 2009.

Section 12

Who is most affected? Race

In the United States, Black populations have the highest rates of new cancer cases and the highest number of cancer deaths of any racial group. Researchers point to a combination of factors:

Genetics

Some genetic factors can play a role in cancer risk and how certain cancers behave.

Healthcare inequities

Unequal access to high-quality care contributes to gaps in outcomes.

Delayed care

Past experiences of racism within the healthcare system can delay people from seeking care.

Access to state-of-the-art care

Black patients are statistically less likely to receive the most advanced available treatments.

Research representation

Black patients are statistically less likely to be included in clinical research studies, which can affect how well treatments are tailored to them.

Socioeconomic factors

Differences in education and access to health insurance compound the other factors above.

See Maria Aspan's reporting on racial disparities in breast cancer outcomes, Fortune, 2020, for a deeper look at how these factors connect.

Section 13

Cancer close to home: Appalachian Kentucky

Within Kentucky — the state with the nation's highest overall rate of new cancer cases — cancer doesn't affect every region equally either.

  • Rural residents, in general, are more likely to develop cancer than people living in urban areas.
  • Residents of Appalachian Kentucky are about 8% more likely to die from a preventable cancer than people living outside Appalachia.

Why?

Lifestyle habits

Behaviors that increase cancer risk — covered in depth in Module 2 — are more common in the region.

Healthcare access

Distance to providers, transportation, and availability of specialists all play a role — this comes up again in Module 3.

Education & socioeconomic levels

As in Section 10, lower education and income levels are linked to later diagnoses and worse outcomes.

The good news: most of these factors are things that communities, healthcare systems, and individuals can work to change — which is exactly what the rest of this course is about.

Section 14

Module 1 summary

  • Cancer is caused by cells dividing uncontrollably, usually starting with a DNA mutation.
  • Tumors can be benign (not cancerous, can't spread) or malignant (cancerous, can spread).
  • Metastasis is when cancer cells spread to a new part of the body — but the cancer keeps the name of where it started.
  • There are more than 100 types of cancer, usually named for the organ or cell type involved.
  • Cancer is diagnosed through screening and biopsy, followed by examining cells under a microscope.
  • Certain groups — including Black communities, lower-income communities, and residents of Kentucky's Appalachian region — experience higher rates of cancer and worse outcomes.
