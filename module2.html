Section 1

Welcome back

A quick review, then what's ahead

Before diving in, here's a refresher on the big ideas from Module 1:

  • Cancer is caused by uncontrollable cell division, usually starting with a DNA mutation.
  • Tumors can be malignant (cancerous) or benign (not cancerous).
  • Cancer cells can spread to other parts of the body — this is called metastasis.
  • Certain groups see higher numbers of cancer cases, including Black communities, lower socioeconomic groups, and people living in Kentucky — especially Appalachian Kentucky.

What's ahead in Module 2

If some people and places are more affected by cancer than others, what's actually driving that? This module looks at the major risk factors for cancer — some you can change, and some you can't — and ends with a simple framework for lowering your own risk.

Section 4

What is a risk factor?

A risk factor is anything that increases a person's likelihood of developing a disease — in this case, cancer.

Quick question

Are risk factors always within someone's control?

Right. Things like genetics and family history are risk factors, but they're not modifiable behaviors — you can't change your genes. The good news is that most of the risk factors covered in this module can be changed, which is exactly why this module focuses on them.

Section 5

Ten major risk factors

Click each risk factor below to learn what it is, why it matters, and — where relevant — how it connects to Kentucky.

Advancing age is the most significant risk factor for cancer. About one in four new cancer cases are diagnosed in people ages 65–74.

Why? Mutations in a cell's DNA can cause it to begin dividing uncontrollably, and studies show that DNA mutations tend to increase with age — simply because cells have divided (and copied their DNA) more times.

Kentucky connection: the average age in Appalachian Kentucky is about 40.9 — relatively young — which is one reason this module focuses on the risk factors people can influence well before age becomes the dominant factor.

Drinking too much alcohol increases the risk of several cancers, including mouth, throat, larynx, liver, and breast cancer.

Does the type of alcohol matter? No — beer, wine, and hard liquor all contain ethanol, and it's the amount consumed that matters, not the type.

Why? Alcohol is an irritant that can damage cells. As damaged cells try to repair themselves, mistakes in that repair process can lead to the kinds of DNA mutations that cause cancer.

Kentucky connection: Kentucky ranks 17th in the nation for the percentage of adults who drink excessively.

People whose immune systems are underactive — "immunosuppressed" — are more likely to develop cancer.

Why? A weaker immune system is less able to detect and destroy cancer cells before they can grow into tumors.

People who have recently received an organ transplant and are taking immunosuppressive drugs (to prevent their body from rejecting the new organ) are at especially high risk.

Long-term exposure to certain substances in the environment — called carcinogens — can increase cancer risk. Common examples include air pollution, radon, asbestos, engine exhaust, and heavy metals.

Why? Some carcinogens directly cause DNA mutations. Others cause cells to divide faster, which increases the chances that a mutation will occur during one of those extra divisions.

Kentucky connection: coal is an important resource and industry in Kentucky, but too much exposure to coal-related substances can increase a person's risk of developing a harmful mutation.

Increased levels of estrogen and progesterone increase a woman's risk of developing breast cancer. Women receiving hormone replacement therapy are at especially high risk.

Why? Higher hormone levels can speed up how often cells multiply, which increases the odds that a DNA mutation will occur along the way.

Being significantly overweight increases the risk for many diseases, including cancers of the breast, colon, rectum, endometrium, esophagus, kidney, pancreas, and gallbladder, among others.

Why?

  • Excess fat can deprive cells of oxygen, triggering inflammation. Long-term inflammation can damage cells and make mutations more likely.
  • High insulin levels (common with obesity) can stimulate cell division and suppress normal cell death — both of which favor tumor growth.
  • High cytokine levels (also common with obesity) can drive inflammation and mutations.

Kentucky connection: Kentucky has the 10th-highest obesity rate in the country — about 41% of adults.

Excessive exposure to radiation — including X-rays, gamma rays, and neutrons — increases cancer risk.

Why? Radiation is known to damage DNA, creating mutations that can lead to cancer.

This is why patients receiving X-rays wear a lead vest — the lead reduces the amount of radiation that reaches the rest of the body.

Like radiation, too much exposure to ultraviolet (UV) light — from natural sunlight or artificial sources like tanning beds — increases cancer risk.

Why? UV rays damage skin cells, which can cause the kinds of mutations that lead to uncontrolled cell division.

Kentucky connection: it's common for Kentucky farmers and other outdoor workers to skip sunscreen during long days of physical work — increasing their cumulative UV exposure over time.

Tobacco use is a leading cause of cancer — and of death from cancer.

  • People who smoke are regularly exposed to tobacco smoke and its chemicals.
  • People who live around smokers are exposed to secondhand smoke.
  • People who use smokeless (chewing) tobacco have an increased risk of mouth and esophagus cancers.

Why? Tobacco products contain many chemicals that damage DNA and create mutations.

Kentucky connection: in Kentucky, about 19.6% of adults smoke (4th highest in the nation), 5.3% of high school students smoke, and 19.7% of high school students use e-vapor (vaping) products.

Certain viruses, bacteria, and parasites can increase cancer risk — for example, Epstein-Barr virus, hepatitis, HIV, and HPV.

Why?

  • Some viruses disrupt the cell signaling that normally controls cell division, leading to uncontrolled growth.
  • Some infections weaken the immune system, making it harder for the body to fight off cancer-causing infections.
  • Some infectious agents cause chronic inflammation, which increases the chance of a mutation occurring.
Section 6

What can you do? The three A's

Knowing the risk factors is useful — but what can you actually do about them? This module groups practical steps into three categories.

1. Avoid

Avoid

Stay away from substances and behaviors known to increase cancer risk:

  • Tobacco
  • Excessive sun exposure (including tanning beds)
  • Excessive alcohol
  • Environmental carcinogens, where possible
2. Develop

Develop

Build lifestyle habits that decrease cancer risk:

  • A sustainable exercise routine
  • A healthy diet

These habits also help prevent obesity — lowering risk for many other diseases at the same time.

3. Act

Act

Take action to improve cancer knowledge and catch problems early:

  • Seek out informative books, pamphlets, and presentations — and share what you learn with friends and family
  • Schedule medical appointments and follow your doctor's recommendations for check-ups
  • Attend preventive screenings, which can catch cancer before it spreads and greatly improve survival
Section 7

Module 2 summary

  • A risk factor is anything that increases the likelihood of developing a disease — some can be changed, and some (like genetics) can't.
  • Major modifiable risk factors for cancer include: old age (not modifiable, but informative), excessive alcohol use, immunosuppression, environmental carcinogens, increased hormone levels, obesity, radiation, excessive sun exposure, tobacco use, and certain infections.
  • You can lower your own risk by following the three A's: Avoid risk-increasing behaviors, Develop a healthy lifestyle, and Act — build your knowledge and use preventive care.
  • Don't forget to share what you learn with others!
