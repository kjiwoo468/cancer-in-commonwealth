Educational tool — not a diagnosis

Cancer Risk Estimator for Appalachian Kentucky

This tool gives a rough, personalized sense of how a few well-studied risk factors line up for the five cancers where Appalachian Kentucky carries the largest share of the nation's burden: lung, colorectal, cervical, laryngeal, and oral cavity/pharyngeal cancer. It compares your inputs to Kentucky's own average, not the national average.

This is an educational estimate, not a medical risk assessment. It's built from published relative-risk research (see How this is calculated below), but it has not been validated against actual patient outcomes the way a clinical tool like the Gail model or PLCOm2012 has. It cannot diagnose cancer, and it isn't a substitute for talking with a doctor — especially if you have a strong family history of cancer. If a number here worries you, that's worth bringing to a doctor; please don't let it cause distress on its own.

Your estimate

Each card shows your estimated risk as a multiple of the average Kentuckian's risk for that cancer — not a percent chance of getting cancer.

This tool follows the same relative-risk logic used by the Harvard Cancer Risk Index / Your Disease Risk, but anchors the comparison to Kentucky's own average instead of the U.S. average, and adds a few Appalachian-Kentucky-specific inputs (radon, coal-mining history, HPV vaccination, and regional screening gaps) that national tools don't ask about. It is documented in full, including every citation below, in the accompanying research paper, A Regionally Calibrated Approach to Cancer Risk Estimation for Appalachian Kentucky. Values marked approx. are reasoned interpolations between published estimates rather than a single directly cited number — the paper explains each one.

  • Current smoking & lung cancer: ~20× (lifetime average)
  • Secondhand smoke, never-smokers, lung cancer: 1.24×
  • Coal-mining employment, lung cancer: ~1.3× approx.
  • BMI and colorectal/cervical cancer: 1.10× per 5 kg/m²
  • Least- vs. most-active, colorectal cancer: ~1.3× approx.
  • Heavy alcohol use, colorectal cancer: 1.44×
  • Processed meat, colorectal cancer: 1.18× per 50 g/day
  • First-degree family history, colorectal cancer: ~2.5× approx.
  • HPV vaccination before exposure, cervical cancer: up to ~90% risk reduction
  • Current smoking, oral/pharyngeal cancer: 3.58×; former smoking: 1.61×
  • Heavy alcohol use, oral/pharyngeal cancer: 5.13×
  • Current smoking, laryngeal cancer: 7.0× (isolated from alcohol)
  • Heavy alcohol use, laryngeal cancer: 2.65×

To avoid double-counting how common some of these factors already are in Kentucky (for example, about 1 in 4 adults already smoke), the calculator first works out an implied \u201creference\u201d rate for someone with none of the region's common risk factors, then rebuilds an individual estimate from your specific answers. Full derivation and limitations are in the research paper. This tool assumes risk factors act independently, which is a simplification — in reality several of them (like smoking and heavy drinking) tend to cluster together.

About the \u201coverall\u201d number: it's a weighted average of the five site-specific estimates, weighted by how common each cancer actually is (using national SEER incidence rates as the weighting basis, since precise site-by-site Appalachian Kentucky rates aren't available for all five). That weighting means a large multiplier on a rare cancer like larynx (about 2.5 cases per 100,000 people nationally) moves the overall number much less than the same size multiplier on a common one like lung (about 47 per 100,000) or colorectal cancer (about 38 per 100,000) — which mirrors how these risks actually add up in real life. It is a combined estimate across only the five cancers this tool covers, not a general \u201ccancer risk\u201d figure; most cancer types (breast, prostate, skin, kidney, and dozens of others) aren't modeled here at all, mainly because they aren't where Appalachian Kentucky's documented excess burden is concentrated (see the research paper, Section 3).
