Section 3

How is cancer diagnosed?

A patient might come in with a lump or another symptom — but the cells involved have to be examined closely before anyone can say whether it's cancer.

1

Scan

Cancer is often first identified using an imaging scan.

2

Biopsy

A doctor performs a biopsy: removing a sample of the tissue in question and sending it to a lab.

3

Microscope exam

An expert examines the sample under a microscope to determine whether it's cancerous — the kind of comparison you made back in Module 1.

4

Treatment begins

Once cancer is confirmed, doctors can begin planning treatment — the focus of the rest of this module.

Section 4

Why early detection matters

Early detection means cancer is found while it's still in its early stages: it's small, it hasn't spread, and there are usually few or no symptoms yet. Early detection is achieved through regular screening tests for specific cancer types.

Which is easier to cure?

Compare these two scenarios:

Scenario A

A cancer that is large and has spread to different areas of the body.

Scenario B

A cancer that is small and has not yet spread.

Which is generally easier to treat successfully?

Right. Early detection improves a patient's chances of survival. A small, localized cancer is generally far easier to treat — sometimes with a single treatment like surgery — than a cancer that has already spread to multiple parts of the body.

Actually, it's Scenario B. Early detection improves a patient's chances of survival — a small, localized cancer is generally far easier to treat than one that has already spread.

Section 5

What happens if early detection doesn't occur?

Patients living in rural areas are more likely to be diagnosed with later-stage cancers than patients living in urban areas — often because of a lack of access to healthcare services. A later-stage diagnosis can affect a patient's entire treatment plan.

Think about it

Before reading on: what parts of Kentucky do you think might have limited access to cancer care, and why?

Barriers for rural Appalachian Kentucky residents

Distance

Many residents live very far from the nearest cancer care facility.

Road conditions

The roads connecting rural areas to medical centers can be winding and dangerous — especially in bad weather.

Time off work

Traveling for care often means taking time off work, which isn't always possible.

Section 6

Eight types of cancer treatment

There isn't one single "cancer treatment" — doctors choose from (and often combine) several different approaches depending on the cancer type, its stage, and the patient. Click each one to learn more.

One of the most common cancer treatments. Chemotherapy uses powerful chemicals (drugs) to kill cancer cells.

  • May involve a single drug, or multiple drugs infused through an IV.
  • When multiple drugs are used, they're often given in a specific order.
  • The drugs chosen depend on the tumor's size, the cancer's stage, the patient's age, and the patient's overall health.

Radiation therapy uses high doses of radiation — the same general technology used to take an X-ray of a broken bone — to damage the DNA of cancer cells, killing them and shrinking tumors.

Radiation doesn't work immediately: it can take days or weeks of treatment before cancer cells begin to die, and cells continue dying even after treatment ends.

A surgeon removes the tumor from a specific part of the body — either the entire tumor, or just part of it.

Doctors may remove only part of a tumor if removing all of it would damage another part of the body. Removing some or all of a tumor can also help other treatments work better, and can relieve pain or pressure caused by the tumor.

Immunotherapy is a treatment that helps a patient's own immune system better recognize and fight cancer. It's not as widely used as surgery, chemotherapy, or radiation therapy.

It can be given in several forms: through an IV, as a pill, as a topical ointment, or inserted directly into the bladder (for certain bladder cancers).

Stem cells are young cells that can develop into nearly any cell type in the body.

A stem cell transplant restores stem cells in patients whose own stem cells were destroyed by chemotherapy or radiation. This helps restore the body's supply of white blood cells, red blood cells, and platelets.

Targeted therapy aims at specific proteins that control how cancer cells grow, divide, and spread. Depending on the drug, it can:

  • Help the immune system destroy cancer cells
  • Stop cancer cells from growing
  • Deliver cell-killing substances directly to cancer cells
  • Starve cancer cells of the hormones they need to grow

Hormone therapy slows or stops the growth of certain cancers by changing the hormones available to them. It can be used to stop cancer growth or to ease symptoms.

It's only used to treat patients with prostate or breast cancer — cancers whose growth can be influenced by hormone levels.

With precision medicine, doctors choose a treatment based on a genetic understanding of the patient's specific tumor.

Each tumor type has a genetic change that allows it to grow and spread. Patients whose tumors share that genetic change receive one type of treatment, while patients with a different genetic change receive a different treatment.

This approach only works if the tumor has a genetic change that can actually be targeted by an existing drug.

Section 7

Why treatment plans differ between patients

Quick question

True or false: the same treatment works equally well for every patient with the same type of cancer.

Correct — false. Some treatments work for some patients and not for others, even with the same general type of cancer.

Not quite — the statement is false. Some treatments work for some patients and not for others, even with the same general type of cancer.

Three big factors explain why:

Genetics

A patient's individual genetic makeup can affect how their body responds to a given treatment.

Drug & treatment interactions

How a drug or treatment interacts with a patient's body — including other medications they take — varies from person to person.

Cancer & tumor type

The specific type of cancer and tumor — including its genetic characteristics — affects which treatments are even an option.

Section 8

Combination therapy

Combination therapy is when a doctor combines two or more drugs or treatment types. This can mean combining different kinds of treatment — for example, surgery and radiation, or radiation and chemotherapy — or combining different drugs within a single type of treatment, such as using three different drugs during chemotherapy.

Advantages

  • Increases the likelihood the cancer will be eliminated
  • Helps prevent the cancer from developing drug resistance
  • Can reduce the overall length of treatment

Disadvantages

  • It's not always clear how a patient's body will respond to two treatment types at once
  • It's sometimes unclear how the two treatments or drugs will interact with each other
Section 9

Module 3 summary

  • Cancer is typically diagnosed with a scan, followed by a biopsy and examination under a microscope.
  • Early detection is key to survival — a small, localized cancer is generally much easier to treat than one that has already spread.
  • Barriers to early detection — especially in rural Appalachian Kentucky — include distance to care, road conditions, and the ability to take time off work.
  • There are eight major types of cancer treatment: chemotherapy, radiation therapy, surgery, immunotherapy, stem cell transplant, targeted therapy, hormone therapy, and precision medicine — each working differently and carrying different side effects.
  • Treatment plans differ between patients because of genetics, how drugs interact with the body, and the specific cancer/tumor type.
  • Combination therapy — using two or more treatments together — has real advantages, but also some uncertainties.
